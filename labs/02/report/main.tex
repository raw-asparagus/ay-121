\title{AY121 Lab 2: 21-cm Line Calibration, Velocity Referencing, and Cable Characterization}
\author{Jun Rui Ting \\ March 5, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
I present an end-to-end analysis of AY121 Lab 2 using dual-LO frequency-switched observations of Galactic HI near 1420.4058 MHz and a companion transmission-line calibration experiment. For the zenith line-shape dataset, I remove instrumental bandpass structure with spectral division and fit the extracted line profile with a two-component Gaussian model plus a linear baseline. The fitted topocentric centroids are $+5.03\pm0.02$ km s$^{-1}$ and $-34.65\pm0.06$ km s$^{-1}$ in the 1420 MHz bandpass ratio profile, with reduced chi-square values of $\chi_r^2=1.52$ (1420-band profile) and $\chi_r^2=1.38$ (1421-band reciprocal profile). Applying the projected Local Standard of Rest correction $\Delta v_{\rm LSR}=-28.7377$ km s$^{-1}$ shifts these components to approximately $-23.7$ and $-63.4$ km s$^{-1}$. A hot/cold Y-factor calibration gives receiver temperatures of $T_{\rm rx}=176.84\pm0.04\,(\mathrm{meas})\pm17.40\,(\mathrm{total})$ K at 1420 MHz and $208.27\pm0.04\,(\mathrm{meas})\pm5.27\,(\mathrm{total})$ K at 1421 MHz. In a condensed Week-2 cable analysis, the shared-slope linear attenuation fit yields $\alpha=0.951\pm0.175$ dB m$^{-1}$ at 1420 MHz (95\% bootstrap CI $[0.619,1.459]$ dB m$^{-1}$), with reflection-aware ripple fits retained in the appendix for model comparison.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Pointing Formalism and Coordinate Pipeline}
\label{sec:pointing_formalism}

My science targets are usually specified in Galactic coordinates $(l,b)$ because
the measurement objective is Milky Way gas structure. I then move through
equatorial coordinates $(\alpha,\delta)$, which are Earth-location agnostic and
convenient for time-dependent sky referencing, before converting to local
horizontal coordinates $(az,alt)$ required to physically point the horn.

For New Campbell Hall I use the site defaults in the analysis code:
$\phi=37.873199^\circ$ (latitude), $\lambda=-122.257063^\circ$ (longitude),
and $h=120$ m (altitude). Pointing is time dependent through the local sidereal
time, so the same $(l,b)$ target maps to different $(az,alt)$ at different JD.

The matrix method follows the coordinate handout. First map angles to a unit
vector,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point_vec}
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos(lat)\cos(long)\\
\cos(lat)\sin(long)\\
\sin(lat)
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
with $(long,lat)=(l,b)$ in the Galactic frame. Then apply the rotation chain
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point_gal2eq}
\mathbf{x}_{eq}=R_{eq\leftarrow gal}\mathbf{x}_{gal},
\end{equation}
where $R_{eq\leftarrow gal}=R_{gal\leftarrow eq}^{T}$ and
$R_{gal\leftarrow eq}$ is the J2000 matrix from the lab handout. Next convert
equatorial to hour-angle coordinates using $ha=LST-\alpha$ and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point_eq2ha}
\mathbf{x}_{ha}=R_{(ha,\delta)\leftarrow(\alpha,\delta)}\mathbf{x}_{eq}.
\end{equation}
Finally convert to horizontal coordinates with site latitude $\phi$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point_ha2altaz}
R_{(az,alt)\leftarrow(ha,\delta)}=
\begin{bmatrix}
-\sin\phi & 0 & \cos\phi\\
0 & -1 & 0\\
\cos\phi & 0 & \sin\phi
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
\mathbf{x}_{hor}=R_{(az,alt)\leftarrow(ha,\delta)}\mathbf{x}_{ha}.
\end{equation}
Recover $(az,alt)$ from $\mathbf{x}_{hor}$ via
$az=\operatorname{atan2}(x_1,x_0)$ and $alt=\arcsin(x_2)$.

In practice this workflow is implemented in
\texttt{ugradiolab.utils.compute\_pointing} and used by
\texttt{labs/02/scripts/standard.py} for the lab target
$(l,b)=(120^{\circ},0^{\circ})$. I cross-check these transforms against
\texttt{astropy}; forward/inverse consistency is enforced through rotation-matrix
transpose identities.

For zenith observations, RA is approximated by local sidereal angle and Dec by
observatory latitude; this gives $\mathrm{RA}=62.897^{\circ}$ and
$\mathrm{Dec}=37.873^{\circ}$ for the Doppler projection call.

\section{Week 1 Calibration Pipeline: Ratio, Gain Scale, and Y-Factor}
\label{sec:week1_cal}

\subsection{Bandpass cancellation by spectral division}

Let $s_{\rm on}(\nu)$ and $s_{\rm off}(\nu)$ be long-integration spectra with
HI line present and shifted.
Following the handout workflow,
\begin{equation}
  r_{\rm line}(\nu)=\frac{s_{\rm on}(\nu)}{s_{\rm off}(\nu)}.
\end{equation}
Because both spectra traverse the same instrumental chain,
first-order multiplicative bandpass structure cancels in the ratio.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{figures/ratio_profile_lsr.png}
\caption{Bandpass-calibrated HI ratio profiles in the LSR frame.
The two LO views produce complementary peak/dip signatures from the same sky line.}
\label{fig:ratio_profile}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Intensity scale and receiver temperature cross-check}

The lab handout gain scale is
\begin{equation}
  G =
  \frac{T_{\rm sys,cal}-T_{\rm sys,cold}}
  {\sum (s_{\rm cal}-s_{\rm cold})}
  \sum s_{\rm cold},
\end{equation}
with calibrated spectrum
\begin{equation}
  T_{\rm line}(\nu)=r_{\rm line}(\nu)\,G.
\end{equation}

As an independent check, I compute Y-factors from hot/cold datasets.
Results are:
\begin{itemize}
\item 1420 MHz: $Y=2.353705\pm0.000226$ ($3.7175\pm0.0004$ dB),
$T_{\rm rx}=176.840\pm0.035\,(\mathrm{meas})\pm17.403\,(\mathrm{total})$ K.
\item 1421 MHz: $Y=2.352864\pm0.000226$ ($3.7160\pm0.0004$ dB),
$T_{\rm rx}=208.273\pm0.037\,(\mathrm{meas})\pm5.270\,(\mathrm{total})$ K.
\end{itemize}

The larger total uncertainty at 1420 MHz is dominated by assumed hot/cold
load systematics rather than random power-estimator uncertainty.

\section{Velocity Axes and LSR Correction}
\label{sec:velocity_lsr}

I use radio-definition Doppler velocity
\begin{equation}
  v \approx c\,\frac{\nu_0-\nu}{\nu_0},
\end{equation}
with $\nu_0=1420.405751768$ MHz.
The projected correction from
\texttt{ugradio.doppler.get\_projected\_velocity} is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta v_{\rm LSR}=-28.7377\ \mathrm{km\ s^{-1}},
\end{equation}
so I report
\begin{equation}
  v_{\rm LSR}=v_{\rm topo}+\Delta v_{\rm LSR}.
\end{equation}

\section{Condensed Cable/Termination Results (Main Text)}
\label{sec:cable_summary}

Following the Week-2 cable calibration activity,
I estimate SDR-referenced attenuation at fixed RF frequency near the HI line.
After excluding the known long-length anomaly ($L\approx7.925$ m),
a shared-slope linear fit to 8 sets gives
\begin{equation}
  \alpha = 0.951\pm0.175\ \mathrm{dB\ m^{-1}},
\end{equation}
with 95\% bootstrap interval
$[0.619,1.459]$ dB m$^{-1}$.
The two LO intercepts differ by
$\Delta B=-0.551\pm0.210$ dB.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The dual-LO ratio method is effective: independent line-profile reconstructions
agree in centroid and width, and both produce physically plausible multi-component
HI structure.
The LSR shift is large enough ($\sim29$ km s$^{-1}$) that reporting only topocentric
velocities would hinder reproducibility and comparison across observing dates.

The largest calibration limitations in this draft are:
(1) systematic uncertainty in absolute temperature scaling,
(2) residual baseline mismatch not fully captured by a first-order polynomial,
and (3) cable-model ambiguity (linear-only vs reflection-aware forms).

\section{Cable Model and Sensitivity Details}
\label{sec:app_cable}

The attenuation notebook compares two families:
\begin{align}
  y(L) &= B - \alpha L \quad\text{(linear)}\\
  y(L) &= B - \alpha L + A\cos(kL+\phi) \quad\text{(reflection-aware)}.
\end{align}

For SDR data, fit metrics were:
\begin{itemize}
\item Linear: RMSE $=0.3788$ dB, AIC $=-25.067$, BIC $=-22.749$.
\item Ripple: RMSE $=0.1245$ dB, AIC $=-50.667$, BIC $=-44.486$.
\end{itemize}

Ripple-model best-fit parameters include
$\alpha=1.355546$ dB m$^{-1}$,
$k=7.803548$ rad m$^{-1}$,
period $=0.8052$ m,
and amplitudes
$A_{1420}=1.4809$ dB,
$A_{1421}=0.2517$ dB.
However, bootstrap intervals on ripple parameters are wide for this small
length set, so I keep the linear slope as the main reported calibration and
use ripple fits as a systematic-model bracket.
