\title{Observing the Galactic HI 21-cm Line with the Big Horn Receiver}
\author{Jun Rui Ting \\ \today\vspace{-2.5\baselineskip}}

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
We present a complete calibration-first analysis of the Big Horn
21-cm receiver system on the roof of New Campbell Hall, spanning equipment characterization, receiver noise temperature
measurement, and Galactic HI line observations.
Coaxial cable attenuation was measured as $\alpha = 0.73 \pm 0.13$\,dB/m
(SDR) and $0.80 \pm 0.03$\,dB/m (power-meter),
with the unknown roof--lab cable length inferred as $\approx 36$--$40$\,m.
Transmission-line reflectometry gave a coax propagation velocity of
$v_{\rm coax}/c = 0.54 \pm 0.10$, broadly consistent with RG-58 specifications.
The SDR operates in a linear regime with 29\,dB of clipping headroom (RMSE = 0.098\,dB).
Y-factor calibration with a 310\,K hot load and 30\,K cold sky yielded
receiver noise temperatures $T_{\rm rx} = 79 \pm 4$\,K at LO\,1420\,MHz
and $96 \pm 4$\,K at LO\,1421\,MHz, dominated by hot/cold load temperature uncertainty.
Dual-LO ($f_{\rm LO} = 1420$, $1421$\,MHz) frequency-switched observations of
the Galactic-plane field $(l,b) = (120^\circ, 0^\circ)$ reveal a rich, multi-component
HI spectrum spanning $-110$ to $+20$\,km\,s$^{-1}$ in LSR velocity,
interpreted as multiple spiral-arm crossings in the Perseus arm region.
A Cygnus-X cross-validation field confirms a dominant feature near
$-16$\,km\,s$^{-1}$ attributed to Local Arm material.

\vspace{2.5\baselineskip}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=======================================================================

The 21-cm hyperfine line of neutral hydrogen (HI) at $\nu_0 = 1420.40575$\,MHz
is the most widely used spectral tracer of the interstellar medium (ISM),
first detected by \citet{ewen1951}.
Because the Milky Way's disk is optically thin to 21-cm radiation,
the line traces the full column of HI along any line of sight and
encodes the radial-velocity distribution of ISM clouds through the
Doppler shift.
The Big Horn antenna at Campbell Hall, UC Berkeley, provides a simple
but complete radio-astronomy signal chain for observing this line with
a small horn and low-cost software-defined radio (RTL-SDR).

This report documents a three-stage workflow, following
\citet{parsons2024bighorn}:
\textbf{(1)} \emph{equipment calibration} characterizing coax attenuation,
propagation velocity, SDR linearity, and the FIR passband response;
\textbf{(2)} \emph{temperature calibration} measuring the receiver noise temperature
via the Y-factor method with hot and cold load references; and
\textbf{(3)} \emph{HI observations} frequency-switched line extraction,
absolute intensity calibration, Doppler/LSR correction, and Gaussian decomposition.

\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theory}
%=======================================================================

\subsection{HI 21-cm Hyperfine Transition}
\label{sec:theory_hi}

The ground state of hydrogen is split by the interaction between the proton
and electron magnetic moments into two hyperfine levels:
the parallel-spin triplet state ($F=1$, higher energy) and the
antiparallel-spin singlet state ($F=0$, lower energy).
The energy splitting corresponds to a photon frequency of
\begin{equation}
  \nu_0 = 1\,420\,405\,751.768\;\mathrm{Hz}.
\end{equation}
The spontaneous emission (21-cm) Einstein $A$-coefficient is
$A_{10} = 2.869 \times 10^{-15}$\,s$^{-1}$, corresponding to a radiative
lifetime of $\sim 11$\,Myr.
Despite the low transition probability, the abundance of HI ($\sim 10^{21}$\,cm$^{-2}$
in the Galactic plane) makes the line bright.
The line was first detected by \citealt{ewen1951} and has since become
the premier tracer of Galactic HI structure \citep{dickey1990}.

In the optically thin regime, the observed line brightness temperature is
\begin{equation}
  T_B(\nu) = T_s \left[1 - e^{-\tau(\nu)}\right] \approx T_s\,\tau(\nu),
\end{equation}
with spin temperature $T_s$ and optical depth $\tau(\nu)$.
% The Warm Neutral Medium (WNM) is characterized by
% $T_s \sim 5000$--$8000$\,K and $n_H \sim 0.1$--$0.5$\,cm$^{-3}$,
% while the Cold Neutral Medium (CNM) has
% $T_s \sim 50$--$150$\,K and $n_H \sim 10$--$100$\,cm$^{-3}$.
% WNM components dominate the line wings and CNM appears as narrow features.

\subsection{Receiver Noise and System Temperature}
\label{sec:theory_noise}

A radiometric channel records power proportional to the system temperature:
\begin{equation}
  P_j = G_j\,T_{\rm sys}(\nu_j),
  \qquad
  T_{\rm sys} = T_{\rm rx} + T_{\rm sky},
\end{equation}
where $G_j$ is the total chain gain and $T_{\rm rx}$ is the
(frequency-dependent) receiver contribution.
For a single-channel noise measurement, the radiometer equation gives
the thermal fluctuation \citealt{wilson2013}
\begin{equation}
  \delta T_{\rm chan} = \frac{T_{\rm sys}}{\sqrt{\Delta\nu\,\tau}},
\end{equation}
with channel bandwidth $\Delta\nu$ and integration time $\tau$.
For this system: $\Delta\nu = 78.125$\,Hz (SDR, 32768-bin grid),
$\Delta\nu_{\rm cal} = 312.5$\,Hz (8192-bin grid),
$\tau = 419.43$\,s (combined-spectrum integration).

\subsection{Y-Factor Calibration}
\label{sec:theory_yfactor}

The Y-factor method measures $T_{\rm rx}$ by comparing power outputs
when the receiver is fed with two known-temperature sources
\citep{friis1944}:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \frac{P_{\rm hot}}{P_{\rm cold}}
    = \frac{T_{\rm hot} + T_{\rm rx}}{T_{\rm cold} + T_{\rm rx}},
\end{equation}
solving for
\begin{equation}
  T_{\rm rx} = \frac{T_{\rm hot} - Y\,T_{\rm cold}}{Y - 1}.
  \label{eq:trx}
\end{equation}
Error propagation through Equation~\eqref{eq:trx} gives
\begin{align}
  \sigma_{T_{\rm rx}}^2 &=
    \left(\frac{\partial T_{\rm rx}}{\partial T_{\rm hot}}\right)^2\!\sigma_{T_h}^2
    + \left(\frac{\partial T_{\rm rx}}{\partial T_{\rm cold}}\right)^2\!\sigma_{T_c}^2
    + \left(\frac{\partial T_{\rm rx}}{\partial Y}\right)^2\!\sigma_Y^2.
\end{align}
The three contributions are measurement, load-temperature, and
hardware-systematic terms, which are decomposed individually in
Section~\ref{sec:temp_cal_results}.

\subsection{Frequency Switching and Bandpass Removal}
\label{sec:theory_freqswitch}

Frequency switching removes the slowly varying receiver bandpass $G_j(\nu)$
by dividing on-source and off-source spectra taken at two LO settings
\citep{parsons2024calintensity}:
\begin{equation}
  R(\nu_k) = \frac{P_{1420}(\nu_k)}{P_{1421}(\nu_k)},
  \qquad
  y_R = R - 1.
\end{equation}
Since the bandpass shape is approximately the same at both LO settings
(same receiver chain), the ratio $R$ cancels $G_j$ and isolates the
spectral line relative to the continuum.
The inverse-ratio variant
\begin{equation}
  y_{\rm inv} = \frac{1}{R} - 1
    = \frac{P_{1421}}{P_{1420}} - 1
\end{equation}
recovers the same line information with a sign flip.
Absolute intensity scale is restored by multiplying $y_R$ by the
system temperature reference computed from the cold-sky spectrum:
\begin{equation}
  T_{\rm line}(\nu_k) = y_R(\nu_k)\,T_{\rm sys,ref}(\nu_k),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  T_{\rm sys,ref}(\nu_k) = P_{\rm obs}(\nu_k)
  \frac{T_{\rm cold} + T_{\rm rx}}{P_{\rm cold,ref}(\nu_k)}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Transmission-Line Theory}
\label{sec:theory_tline}

A coaxial cable is a two-conductor transmission line with
characteristic impedance $Z_0 = 50\,\Omega$ \citep{parsons2024horn}.
When a traveling wave reaches a load impedance $Z_L \neq Z_0$,
a reflection is generated with voltage reflection coefficient
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}.
\end{equation}
For an open circuit ($Z_L \to \infty$): $\Gamma = +1$ (full reflection, same polarity);
for a short circuit ($Z_L = 0$): $\Gamma = -1$ (full reflection, inverted);
for a matched load ($Z_L = Z_0$): $\Gamma = 0$ (no reflection).
The round-trip delay for a cable of length $L$ and propagation velocity $v_p$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t = \frac{2L}{v_p},
  \qquad
  v_p = \frac{2L}{\Delta t}.
\end{equation}
Cable attenuation is modeled as a linear function of length $L$ at fixed frequency:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\rm rx}(L) = B - \alpha L \quad [\mathrm{dB}],
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the attenuation coefficient (dB/m) and $B$ is an intercept.
An unknown-length cable can be inferred from a measured power loss:
\begin{equation}
  L_{\rm unknown} = \frac{B - P_{\rm obs}}{\alpha}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Coordinate Frames and Pointing}
\label{sec:theory_coords}

Target positions in Galactic coordinates $(l, b)$ are converted to
equatorial J2000 $(\alpha, \delta)$ using the IAU 1958 Galactic convention
of \citet{blaauw1960}, carried to the FK5/J2000 equator and equinox
following \citet{murray1989}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{n}_{\rm eq} = M_{\rm G\to J2000}\,\hat{n}_{\rm gal},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{n}_{\rm gal} = (\cos b \cos l,\; \cos b \sin l,\; \sin b)^{\mathsf T}$
and $M_{\rm G\to J2000}$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{bmatrix}
    -0.05487554 &  \phantom{-}0.49410945 & -0.86766614 \\
    -0.87343710 & -0.44482959            & -0.19807639 \\
    -0.48383499 &  \phantom{-}0.74698225 &  \phantom{-}0.45598379
  \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding FK5/J2000 pole parameters are
$(\alpha_{\rm NGP}, \delta_{\rm NGP}) = (192.85948^\circ, 27.12825^\circ)$
and $l_\Omega = 32.93192^\circ$ (equivalently
$l_{\rm NCP} = 122.93192^\circ$).

Given Local Sidereal Time (LST) at longitude $\lambda$, the hour angle is
$H = \mathrm{LST} - \alpha$, and the topocentric altitude and azimuth are
\begin{align}
  \sin(\mathrm{Alt}) &= \sin\delta\sin\phi + \cos\delta\cos\phi\cos H,\\
  \mathrm{Az}
    &= \arctan\!\left(
      \frac{-\cos\delta\sin H}
           {\sin\delta\cos\phi - \cos\delta\cos H\sin\phi}
    \right),
\end{align}
where $\phi = 37.873^\circ$N is the observatory latitude.
The time-standard pipeline is
\begin{equation}
  t_{\rm unix} \xrightarrow{\rm JD} \mathrm{JD} \xrightarrow{\rm GMST}
  \mathrm{GMST} + \lambda \xrightarrow{} \mathrm{LST}.
\end{equation}
Implementations use \texttt{ugradio.timing} for JD/LST conversion.

\subsection{Doppler Effect and LSR Frame}
\label{sec:theory_lsr}

The radio-definition Doppler velocity is
\begin{equation}
  v_{\rm radio} = c\,\frac{\nu_0 - \nu}{\nu_0}.
\end{equation}
Observed velocities are topocentric; to convert to the Local Standard of Rest (LSR),
we subtract the projection of the Sun's peculiar velocity onto the line of sight:
\begin{equation}
  v_{\rm LSR} = v_{\rm topo} + \Delta v_{\rm LSR},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta v_{\rm LSR}$ is computed from the IAU-standard solar apex
($l = 56^\circ$, $b = 23^\circ$, $v = 20$\,km\,s$^{-1}$) and the
pointing metadata via\newline \texttt{ugradio.doppler}.

The horn antenna has aperture dimensions estimated to be roughly $0.80\,\mathrm{m} \times 0.90\,\mathrm{m}$
(area $= 0.72$\,m$^2$). Following the lab handout's rule-of-thumb for a reasonably
well-matched horn \citep{parsons2024bighorn}, we adopt a provisional aperture
efficiency $\eta_{\rm ap} \approx 0.6$--$0.7$; this is an assumed prior rather than
a direct measurement.
The RTL-SDR (RTL2832U chipset) digitizes at 2.56\,MS/s, producing either an
8192-channel FFT grid at $\Delta\nu = 312.5$\,Hz resolution or a 32768-channel
grid at $\Delta\nu = 78.125$\,Hz over the same 2.56\,MHz bandwidth.
The per-observation integration time is 104.86\,s.
The RTL2832U applies a 32-tap symmetric FIR filter as part of its internal
decimation chain to suppress aliasing before FFT channelization; combined with
the firmware's channel-summing operation, this produces a frequency-dependent
passband that rolls off toward the band edges. As such, for line analysis we focus on the flatter central region
within roughly $\pm 0.8$\,MHz of the LO.

\subsection{Observation Geometry and Pointing}

The primary science target is $(l,b) = (120^\circ, 0^\circ)$.

$(\alpha, \delta) = (96.45^\circ, 62.73^\circ)$ in J2000.

\subsection{Cable Attenuation Characterization}
\label{sec:cable_attenuation}

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

To characterize the coaxial cable attenuation coefficient $\alpha$ (dB/m),
power measurements were made through known-length RG-58 cable segments
at both LO frequencies (1420 and 1421\,MHz)
(bench layout: Appendix~\ref{fig:bench_attenuation}).
A shared-slope linear model was fit jointly to both LO datasets:
\begin{equation}
  P_{{\rm rx},i}(L) = B_i - \alpha L, \quad i \in \{1420, 1421\},
\end{equation}
where $B_i$ is a frequency-dependent intercept and $\alpha$ is shared.
One outlier cable run was removed during quality screening
(8 of 9 runs retained based on residual diagnostics).

\subsubsection{Results: SDR Method}

\begin{figure*}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/cable_attenuation_lo.pdf}
  \caption{Normalized SDR power versus cable length for the LO\,1420 and LO\,1421
  bench measurements. The dotted lines show fits using all cable runs, while the
  dashed lines show the adopted shared-slope fit after omitting the 26-ft cable
  outlier marked with $\times$. Dash-dotted lines give representative RG-58
  reference attenuation trends for comparison. The lower two panels show the
  corresponding residuals for the two fit choices. Notably, the fit omitting the
  strong outlier matches well with an RG-58 weather-resistant reference attenuation.}
  \label{fig:cable_attenuation_lo}
\end{figure*}

The SDR-path fit gives:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{\rm SDR} = 0.73 \pm 0.13\;\mathrm{dB/m},
\end{equation}
with a 95\% confidence interval of $[0.47, 0.99]$\,dB/m. 

\subsubsection{Cross-Check: Power-Meter Method}

An independent power-meter measurement of the same cable set yields:
\begin{figure*}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/cable_attenuation_power_meter.pdf}
  \caption{Cable attenuation cross-check using a calibrated power meter.
  The fitted slope $\alpha_{\rm meter} = 0.80 \pm 0.03$\,dB/m is
  statistically consistent with the SDR estimate within $\sim 0.6\sigma$.}
  \label{fig:cable_attenuation_pm}
\end{figure*}

\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{\rm meter} = 0.80 \pm 0.03\;\mathrm{dB/m},
\end{equation}
with a 95\% confidence interval of $[0.74, 0.86]$\,dB/m.
The two estimates differ by $0.07$\,dB/m ($0.6\sigma$), consistent
within uncertainties.
The power-meter value is substantially more precise due to its direct,
analog calibrated measurement versus the SDR's indirect spectral ratio.

\subsubsection{Unknown Roof--Lab Cable Length}

Using the fitted SDR intercept, the unknown roof--lab cable length is inferred as
(Appendix~\ref{fig:bench_unknown_length}):
\begin{equation}
  L_{\rm SDR} = 40 \pm 7\;\mathrm{m}.
\end{equation}
Independent estimates are: $L_{\rm meter} = 36 \pm 1$\,m (power-meter intercept)
and $L_{\rm bench} = 36 \pm 1$\,m (bench analog measurement).
The bench and meter estimates are in excellent agreement, while the SDR estimate is
$\sim 0.55\sigma$ higher.
We adopt $L \approx 36$--$40$\,m as a conservative range,
with $L = 36$\,m as the best point estimate from the more precise analog measurements.

The total attenuation contribution of the 36\,m roof--lab cable at 1420\,MHz is
approximately $36 \times 0.80 \approx 29$\,dB, which is partially compensated
by the three rooftop and two lab amplifiers.

\subsection{Termination States and Coax Propagation Velocity}
\label{sec:reflectometry}

\subsubsection{Reflectometry Measurement}

A square-wave generator was connected to one end of a coaxial cable
of known length $L = 40$\,m (primary reflectometry branch;
bench layout: Appendix~\ref{fig:bench_reflectometry}).
The reflected echo is observed with a modular edge separation
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_{\rm mod} = 98.0\;\mathrm{ns}.
\end{equation}
Because the drive waveform is a 2.5\,MHz square wave with period
$T = 400.0$\,ns, the physical round-trip delay is branch-resolved as
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_{\rm round\text{-}trip}
    = \Delta t_{\rm mod} + nT
    = 498.0\;\mathrm{ns},
\end{equation}
where the $n=1$ branch is the smallest solution consistent with a
coax-like propagation speed for a 40\,m cable.
The coax propagation velocity is therefore
\begin{equation}
  v_{\rm coax} = \frac{2L}{\Delta t}
    = 1.609 \times 10^8\;\mathrm{m/s},
\end{equation}
corresponding to a velocity factor
\begin{equation}
  v_{\rm coax}/c = 0.54 \pm 0.10.
\end{equation}
For the Pasternack RG58C/U cable used here, the nominal velocity factor is
$v/c = 0.659$ \citep{pasternack_rg58cu};
our measurement is $\sim 1.2\sigma$ below this, but still consistent
within uncertainty.

\begin{figure*}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/reflectometry.pdf}
  \caption{Square-wave reflectometry trace showing the incident signal
  and reflected pulse from the open cable end ($\Gamma = +1$).
  The measured modular edge separation is $\Delta t_{\rm mod} = 98.0$\,ns;
  with the 2.5\,MHz square-wave period $T = 400.0$\,ns and branch $n=1$,
  this gives the physical round-trip delay $\Delta t = 498.0$\,ns and
  $v_{\rm coax}/c = 0.54 \pm 0.10$.}
  \label{fig:reflectometry}
\end{figure*}

\subsubsection{Termination States}

The cable was tested in four states to characterize impedance behavior:
\textit{open end} ($\Gamma = +1$, full positive reflection),
\textit{50\,$\Omega$ matched termination} ($\Gamma \approx 0$, minimal reflection),
\textit{SDR-connected unpowered}, and
\textit{SDR-connected powered}.
The terminated state showed the expected near-zero reflection, confirming
proper 50\,$\Omega$ source impedance.
The SDR input impedance in both powered and unpowered states showed small
but non-negligible reflections, indicating slight impedance mismatch at 1420\,MHz
even though the SMA connector on the SDR is designed for 50\,$\Omega$ impedance.

\subsection{SDR Characterization}
\label{sec:sdr_char}

\subsubsection{Gain Linearity}

The RTL-SDR gain response was swept over a range of input power levels
to characterize the linear operating regime
(bench layout: Appendix~\ref{fig:bench_gain_sweep}).
The key results are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Highest unclipped gain setpoint: 13.0\,dBm
  \item First clipped setpoint: 15.5\,dBm
  \item Clipping headroom: 29.0\,dB (from the observed sky power level)
  \item Linearity RMSE in linear regime: 0.1\,dB
\end{itemize}
As such, sky observations should operate well within a linear gain response.

The RTL2832U digitizer includes a 32-tap symmetric FIR decimation filter.
Rather than measuring this response with a swept tone, we constructed a
synthetic hardware-response diagnostic from the published FIR coefficients,

applied the combined response to a noise input on the output-frequency grid.
Figure~\ref{fig:sdr_fir} compares two summing coefficients: Prof. Aaron Parsons's

  \item Science analysis should be safe to focus on the flatter central region

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Calibration Procedure}
\label{sec:temp_cal_procedure}

The Y-factor method \citep{friis1944} was used to measure
the receiver noise temperature using two calibration loads
(setup: Appendix~\ref{fig:bench_yfactor}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hot load:} A human body (hand blocking the horn aperture) at
        $T_{\rm hot} = 310 \pm 5$\,K.
  \item \textbf{Cold load:} Cold sky pointed toward a low HI column region
        ($l \approx 165^\circ$, $b \approx 36^\circ$) assumed at
        $T_{\rm cold} = 30 \pm 2$\,K.
\end{itemize}
The cold reference was observed just before hot measurements to minimize
gain drift or sky variability due to weather.
Per-channel $T_{\rm rx}(\nu)$ profiles were computed by applying
Equation~\eqref{eq:trx} to each spectral channel independently,
then averaged to obtain the broadband
$T_{\rm rx}$ values.

\subsection{Joint Baseline and Gaussian Decomposition}
\label{sec:gaussian_fits}

A multi-component Gaussian models were fit jointly with a low-order
polynomial baseline to both the $R$-branch ($y_R$ profile) and the
$R_{\rm inv}$ branch ($y_{\rm inv}$ profile) for each field.
The model is
\begin{equation}
  y(v) = \sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k \exp\!\left[-\frac{(v-\mu_k)^2}{2\sigma_k^2}\right]
         + \sum_{m=0}^{M} c_m\,(v/100)^m,
\end{equation}
with FWHM $= 2\sqrt{2\ln 2}\,\sigma_k$, constrained to
$3\,\mathrm{km/s} \leq \mathrm{FWHM} \leq 35\,\mathrm{km/s}$.
Additionally, to prevent fitting unphysical solutions, component amplitudes were
required to remain non-negative, centroids were confined to the adopted
velocity fit window, and widths were bounded to the range above. This
trades a formally lower-$\chi^2_\nu$ unconstrained solution for one whose
peak amplitudes, centroid velocities, and FWHM values remain physically
interpretable as HI components.
The model grid included $N \in \{2,3,4\}$ components and polynomial orders
$M \in \{1,2\}$. All selected solutions used four Gaussian components.
Path A selected $M=2$ in all four cases, while Path B selected $M=1$ for the
Cygnus-X $R$ branch and $M=2$ elsewhere.
Figures~\ref{fig:gaussian_fits_standard} and~\ref{fig:gaussian_fits_cyg} show
the selected joint Gaussian+baseline decompositions for the standard and
Cygnus-X fields, respectively.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=======================================================================

\subsection{Receiver Noise Temperature}

The measured receiver noise temperatures ($T_{\rm rx} = 79$\,K at 1420\,MHz and
$96$\,K at 1421\,MHz) are reasonable for a system using consumer-grade
RTL-SDR components combined with low-noise amplifiers.
Typical horn-antenna receiver systems span 50--300\,K \citep{wilson2013};
our values are in the lower-middle of this range.

% The 17\,K difference between the two LO settings is consistent with
% frequency-dependent gain variation across the $\sim 1$\,MHz LO spacing.
% The RTL2832U analog section has $\sim 5$--$10\%$ gain variation across the
% IF passband, and the LNA (ZKL-33ULN-S+) gain also varies slightly near 1420\,MHz.
% This LO-dependent $T_{\rm rx}$ motivates using the lower-noise LO\,1420 as
% the reference for intensity calibration wherever possible.

\subsection{Cable Characterization}

The SDR attenuation estimate ($\alpha = 0.73$\,dB/m) and power-meter
estimate ($\alpha = 0.80$\,dB/m) agree within $0.6\sigma$.
The standard specification for RG-58 at 1420\,MHz is approximately
$0.5$--$0.8$\,dB/m depending on quality, so both measurements are physically reasonable.

The unknown roof--lab cable length estimates converge on $\sim 36$\,m
from the direct methods (bench measurement and power-meter intercept),
while the SDR estimate ($\sim 40$\,m) has much larger uncertainty.
We adopt $L = 36$\,m as our best estimate.
This gives a total cable attenuation of $36 \times 0.80 \approx 29$\,dB,
which is compensated by the $3 \times 26 = 78$\,dB of rooftop gain.

The measured $v_{\rm coax}/c = 0.54 \pm 0.10$ is about $1.2\sigma$ below
the nominal RG-58 specification ($v/c = 0.66$), but remains consistent
within uncertainty.
This offset most likely reflects the ambiguity in identifying the
periodic square-wave branch and the exact echo-edge pick.

\subsection{HI Line Structure at $(l,b) = (120^\circ, 0^\circ)$}

The standard field exhibits at least four kinematic components spanning
$v_{\rm LSR} \approx -110$ to $+20$\,km/s.
This multi-component structure is expected for a Galactic-plane sight line
in the second quadrant ($l = 120^\circ$), which intersects multiple spiral arms:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The Local Arm ($v_{\rm LSR} \sim -10$ to $+20$\,km/s) contributes
        the dominant component at $v_0 \approx -16$ to $+18$\,km/s
        depending on the extraction path.
  \item The Perseus Arm ($v_{\rm LSR} \sim -50$ to $-80$\,km/s) contributes
        the broad components near $-53$ to $-71$\,km/s with FWHM $= 35$\,km/s.
  \item A possible outer arm feature appears near $-90$ to $-108$\,km/s.
\end{itemize}
The exact velocity assignments depend on the adopted Galactic rotation model.
These velocities are broadly consistent with HI4PI survey values for this
sight line \citep{hi4pi2016,dickey1990,kalberla2009}.
The Big Horn horn has a large field of view, and neither the effective beam
size nor the beam/aperture efficiency was independently mapped in this lab.
The observed components are therefore beam-averaged over a broad patch of the
Galactic plane and are likely coupled to the surrounding large-scale HI
structure, not just to a few discrete line-of-sight features.

\subsection{Fit Quality and Model Limitations}

The standard field fits show $\chi^2_{\rm red} = 6.14$--$7.14$ for the best 4-component models.
Values $\gg 1$ indicate that the model is imperfect. This could be due insufficient number of Gaussian components where the upper bound of
$N = 4$ in the grid search may not capture all kinematic sub-structure, or residual low-order baseline structure not being fully captured by the jointly fit polynomial among other likely causes.
A less constrained mixture can push
$\chi^2_\nu$ closer to 1, but only by accepting unphysical component peak
amplitudes, centroid velocities, or FWHM values that absorb baseline or
calibration structure. We therefore prefer the physics-aware bounded
solutions even when their reduced $\chi^2$ remains significantly above unity.

The Cygnus-X field achieves $\chi^2_{\rm red} = 2.10$,
closer to unity, suggesting that a less complex kinematic structure along
that sight line is better captured by the same 4-component model.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=======================================================================

In this lab, we performed a 3-part analysis of the Big Horn receiver.

Starting with equipment calibration, we characterized coax attenuation
($\alpha = 0.80$\,dB/m, power-meter), unknown cable length
($L \approx 36$\,m), coax propagation velocity
($v_{\rm coax}/c = 0.54 \pm 0.10$), SDR linearity (29\,dB headroom,
RMSE $= 0.098$\,dB), and FIR+summing passband
(FIR-only edge/center $= 0.237$, analysis band $\pm 0.8$\,MHz). Next, we performed temperature calibration via Y-factor measured
$T_{\rm rx} = 79 \pm 4$\,K (LO\,1420) and
$96 \pm 4$\,K (LO\,1421),
with load-temperature uncertainty dominating.
System noise floors of $\delta T_{\rm chan} = 603.2$ and $697.6$\,mK
were verified via the radiometer equation. With a more understood system, we performed
HI observations at $(l,b) = (120^\circ, 0^\circ)$ revealed
a multi-component HI spectrum spanning $-110$ to $+20$\,km/s LSR,
interpreted as Local Arm, Perseus Arm, and outer-arm gas.
A Cygnus-X cross-validation field confirmed the
$\sim -16$\,km/s Local Arm feature.
Gaussian decomposition (4 components, $\chi^2_{\rm red} = 6.14$--$7.14$)
demonstrated the full analysis pipeline.
